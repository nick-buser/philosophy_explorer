## How to read this DAG

Peirce's thought does not read chronologically. His ideas are scattered
across essays, manuscripts, and letters written over fifty years, and
his best statements of a given idea are often not in the same place as
his first statements of it. The DAG captures the reading dependencies,
not the composition chronology.

**Tiers:**

- **Entry points** — no prerequisites. Accessible, canonical, short.
  Read one of these first, regardless of which path you take.
- **Intermediate** — assume familiarity with at least one entry point.
- **Advanced / specialist** — assume familiarity with the
  intermediate tier and usually the secondary literature.

**Paths** — routes through the DAG tailored to interest:

## Entry points

### *The Fixation of Belief* (1877)

*Popular Science Monthly* 12: 1–15. First of the "Illustrations of the
Logic of Science" series. Introduces Peirce's theory of inquiry:
doubt, belief, and the four methods for settling opinion (tenacity,
authority, a priori, science). Short, accessible, canonical. Start here.

### *How to Make Our Ideas Clear* (1878)

*Popular Science Monthly* 12: 286–302. Second in the series. The
locus classicus of the *pragmatic maxim*: the meaning of a concept
is the sum of its conceivable practical consequences. The most cited
Peirce paper; every pragmatism reading list begins here.

### *What Pragmatism Is* (1905)

*The Monist* 15: 161–181. Peirce's mature self-presentation, written
to distance his view from William James's and to introduce the term
"pragmaticism" — "a word ugly enough to be safe from kidnappers."
Less systematically pedagogical than *How to Make Our Ideas Clear*
but reveals the thirty-year development of the position.

## Pragmatism path

After the entry points, continue with:

### *Issues of Pragmaticism* (1905)

*The Monist* 15: 481–499. Companion to *What Pragmatism Is*.
Applies the pragmatic maxim to specific issues and defends critical
commonsensism.

### Harvard Lectures on Pragmatism (1903)

Seven lectures, delivered at Harvard. Systematic statement of the
post-1898 pragmatism integrated with Peirce's phenomenology,
categories, and logic. Best reading: *The Essential Peirce* vol. 2
ch. 10–16.

### *Prolegomena to an Apology for Pragmaticism* (1906)

*The Monist* 16: 492–546. Dense; introduces the existential graphs as
Peirce's machinery for his late pragmatism. Bridge to the EG path.

### *Chance, Love, and Logic* (1923, Morris R. Cohen ed.)

Posthumous collection, historically important as Frank Ramsey's
entry point into Peirce. Still a coherent thematic introduction.

## Logic path

### *On a New List of Categories* (1867)

*Proceedings of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences* 7: 287–298.
Peirce's first major philosophical paper. Dense; revises Kant's
category table into Firstness, Secondness, Thirdness. Foundational for
everything else — logic, semiotics, metaphysics all trace back to
the categories. Read after at least one entry point.

### *Description of a Notation for the Logic of Relatives, resulting from an amplification of the conceptions of Boole's calculus of logic* (1870)

*Memoirs of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences*. Extends Boole
to relations; the direct ancestor of modern relational algebra. This
paper is what Schröder would later build on. Not light reading, but
pivotal.

### *On the Algebra of Logic* (1880)

*American Journal of Mathematics* 3: 15–57. Development of the logic
of relations; introduces much of the notation that would stabilize
in later work.

### *On the Algebra of Logic: A Contribution to the Philosophy of Notation* (1885)

*American Journal of Mathematics* 7: 180–202. Peirce's most mature
algebraic-logic paper. Includes his treatment of quantification —
**fully general quantification, developed independently of Frege**
(Frege 1879). This is the paper that establishes parity with Frege
on the key technical achievement of late-19th-century logic.

## Existential graphs path

### Lowell Lectures (1903)

Eight lectures given at the Lowell Institute, Boston. Lectures IV and
V introduce the existential graphs at a general audience level. The
most accessible Peirce-written introduction to EGs. Best reading:
*The Essential Peirce* vol. 2.

### *Prolegomena to an Apology for Pragmaticism* (1906)

The published statement on EGs. Dense. Introduces the alpha (propositional)
and beta (first-order) systems; gestures at gamma (modal).

### *Logic of the Future* volumes (Bellucci & Pietarinen, eds., 2019–)

The systematic critical edition of Peirce's EG manuscripts. Volume 1
covers history and applications; subsequent volumes cover specific
manuscript sequences. This edition is **transformational for EG
scholarship** — many manuscripts are in print for the first time.

### Selected manuscripts by Robin catalogue number

For the advanced reader working directly with the Peirce Edition
Project's manuscript catalog:

- Robin MS 280 — "Logical Tracts"
- Robin MS 290 — "The Bedrock Beneath Pragmaticism"
- Robin MS 498–499 — EG manipulations

Most of these are covered by *Logic of the Future* with apparatus.

## Semiotics path

### *On a New List of Categories* (1867)

The categories are the foundation of the semiotic triad. Read first.

### Letters to Lady Victoria Welby (1904–1911)

Collected as *Semiotic and Significs: The Correspondence between
Charles S. Peirce and Victoria Lady Welby* (Hardwick, ed., 1977).
Peirce's most sustained accessible discussion of his mature
semiotic theory. Less systematic than the manuscripts but much
clearer.

### *Logic as Semiotic: The Theory of Signs*

Compiled selection of manuscripts in Buchler, *Philosophical Writings
of Peirce* (1940, reprinted Dover). Standard anthology selection for
the mature semiotic theory. Treat as a starting point; the deep
treatment is in Short (2007) — see
[`secondary-literature.md`](./secondary-literature.md).

## Philosophy of science / metaphysics path

### The 1878 *Popular Science Monthly* series (remaining papers)

After *Fixation of Belief* and *How to Make Our Ideas Clear*, the
series continues:

- *The Doctrine of Chances* — probability and epistemic long run.
- *The Probability of Induction* — justification of induction.
- *The Order of Nature* — regularity, law, inference from nature's
  order.
- *Deduction, Induction, and Hypothesis* — first statement of
  abduction as a third mode.

### *Design and Chance* (1884)

Johns Hopkins lecture. Short; introduces the cosmological motif of
chance as a formative principle.

### The Monist metaphysical series (1891–1893)

A loosely connected sequence:

- *The Architecture of Theories* (1891) — methodology for building
  cosmological theories.
- *The Doctrine of Necessity Examined* (1892) — critique of
  mechanistic determinism.
- *The Law of Mind* (1892) — synechism; continuity of mind with
  nature.
- *Man's Glassy Essence* (1892) — physical continuity, protoplasm.
- *Evolutionary Love* (1893) — agapism; love as cosmological principle.

Read the series together; individual papers are harder to follow in
isolation.

## Editions — where to actually find these

| Edition | Scope | Status | Use for |
|---|---|---|---|
| *The Essential Peirce* vols. 1–2 (Houser & Kloesel, eds., Indiana, 1992 / 1998) | ~100 selected writings, 1867–1913 | Complete (2 vols) | Primary reading collection. Chronological within volume. |
| *Collected Papers of Charles Sanders Peirce* vols. 1–8 (Hartshorne, Weiss, Burks, eds., Harvard, 1931–1958) | Very broad, thematically organized | Complete | Reference; scholarly citations use CP volume.paragraph notation. Flawed editorially (thematic splits fragment arguments). |
| *Writings of Charles S. Peirce: A Chronological Edition* (Peirce Edition Project, Indiana, 1982–) | Chronological, scholarly apparatus | In progress; ~vol. 8, through 1892 | The scholarly standard. Best when you need source accuracy. |
| *Logic of the Future: Writings on Existential Graphs* (Bellucci & Pietarinen, eds., De Gruyter, 2019–) | EG manuscripts with apparatus | In progress; vols. 1–3 published | EG-path advanced reading. |
| *Philosophical Writings of Peirce* (Buchler, ed., Dover, 1940) | Selected writings, thematic | Complete | Convenient single-volume anthology; older but still widely used. |
| *Chance, Love, and Logic* (Cohen, ed., 1923, reprinted) | Selected writings focused on methodology and metaphysics | Complete | Historical interest (Ramsey read this); still useful. |

Citation convention: scholarly work uses `CP n.mmm` for *Collected
Papers* (volume.paragraph) and `EP n: mmm` for *The Essential Peirce*
(volume: page). `W n: mmm` for the chronological *Writings*.
